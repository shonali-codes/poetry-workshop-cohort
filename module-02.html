Module 02 · Week One

Reading Multilingual Poetry

Voices Across Languages
DateSunday, 3 May 2026
Time3:30 PM IST
Duration1.5 hours
Our intention To experience translation as a meeting between two poets and two languages — not as a flat copy of one into the other — and to discover that each mother tongue catches a different facet of the same poem.

Before we meet

Come with one language in mind — the one that feels most intimate to you. It need not be the language you speak most. It is the one that holds your inner weather best. We will return to it toward the end of the session.

No readings are required in advance. Everything we need will be shared in the session.

What we will do together

Today we do not study multiple languages. We study what happens between them. Translation is not a copy — it is a meeting, and every meeting leaves fingerprints.

  1. A welcome and framing — translation as hospitality, not as duplication.
  2. A Kabir doha read aloud in its original Hindi, its sound allowed to land before any meaning-making.
  3. Tagore's English translation of the same doha, read alongside — what was preserved, what was let go, where the translator's fingerprints are visible.
  4. A brief pause to breathe.
  5. An introduction to Brajabuli — the invented literary tongue of the Vaishnava poets, which was never anyone's mother tongue. A language born for devotional poetry alone.
  6. A live translation exercise — each of us takes a short Brajabuli verse and renders it into the language we feel can hold it best.
  7. A sharing aloud — three or four of us read our renderings, noticing what each language held differently. No ranking, no correction — only noticing.

The invitation you will carry home

Choose a poem you love — any poem, any language, any era. Sit with it. Then translate it into whichever of your languages you feel can hold it best. Not the language you speak most. The language the poem wants. Write a few sentences about why that language, and what you had to let go of to fit it there. We will share these with each other — with your consent — because translation work wants to be seen.
