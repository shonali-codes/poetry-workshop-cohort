Module 03 · Week Two

Poetry Debates & Discussions

The Kavi Ladai Tradition
DateSaturday, 9 May 2026
Time3:30 PM IST
Duration1.5 hours
Our intention To practise disagreement as craft — not shouting, not silence, but spoken-verse response-to-response — and to discover that a well-held debate deepens both speakers rather than producing a winner.

Before we meet

No preparation required. Come with openness. This is a form many of us have never tried — a spoken poetic exchange between two people on a shared topic, where each line responds to the last. We will approach it gently, together.

If the thought makes you nervous, hold this in mind: the form itself protects you. Kavi Ladai does not reward volume or aggression. It rewards wit, listening, and the line that makes the other person pause. Bring curiosity. That is enough.

What we will do together

We begin by watching an authentic Bengali Kabigan performance together. Then we meet the tradition historically — the medieval Kabiyal lineage, figures like Dashrathi Ray and Anthony Firingi, the ways this form carried philosophy and theology to the unlettered.

  1. A welcome — a short frame on what Kavi Ladai is and why it still matters.
  2. A Kavi Ladai clip watched together — let it play, let it land.
  3. Brief cohort reactions — what did you hear?
  4. Historical context — medieval Bengal, the Kabiyal lineage, how this form carried dissent and devotion both.
  5. Why this still matters — creative conflict resolution, perspective-taking, and the Indian tradition's distinction between vada (dialogue for truth) and vitanda (dialogue only to destroy).
  6. A choice of topics — four options, two light and two weighty. The cohort picks.
  7. A live spoken Kavi Ladai — in pairs, gently facilitated. Not performance. Practice.
  8. A short cohort reflection — not analysis, only noticing. What was in your body?

The invitation you will carry home

For homework, simply share how that felt. Not what you thought. What you felt — in your body, in your breath, in the pauses between responses. Two or three sentences. No analysis. Only noticing.
